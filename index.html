Future of Retail

This is a 10-week capstone project, and we’ve partnered with a Senior UX Researcher from Microsoft Internet of Things design research team and the heads of the retail practice at Point B to use design and technology to rethink the in-store experience.

Particularly, our team is passionate about how design can be used to shape the food and cooking culture. We identified a unique opportunity to build on the Sur La Table mission, and improve their in-store experience by introducing a meaningful use of technology to help them connect the dots in their omni-channel strategy.

Design Process

Generative Research

Research Synthesis

We used several frameworks to synthesize our secondary research in different perspectives and also created competitive landscape to help us analyze current cookware retail industry competitors. We utilized affinity diagram to analyze quantitative data from observations and customer intercepts, and created a customer journey map that outlines the phases of a typical shopping experience, from when customer first start thinking about purchasing to after they've taken at home and are using it.

Customer Journey Map

We identified areas of opportunity at two specific points:

  • While browsing: helping people get inspired, making customers feel good about purchasing products today, and introducing new products and tools
  • After purchase: reducing buyer’s remorse, supporting that feeling of excitement of having purchased a new product

    • Design Question

    How can we improve the in store experience to help shoppers discover new products and realize projects at home?

    Audience

    Existing Sur la Table customers who browse stores for inspiration, seek new ways to up their cooking game.

    Ecosystem Diagram

    We thought about the whole ecosystem to better understand user needs and all the interactions within the cookware retail space.

    Ideation and Design

    We engaged in traditional activities such as brainstorming and grouping our ideas in an affinity diagram to see patterns. Additional techniques and frameworks that helped us arrive at our top ideas included considering, “what if we could make the retail experience more like ___________”. Then we evaluated ideas based on the design principles informed by our research. Scoping down to 2-3 ideas, we got back into stores to get practical insights into how our designs might be realized in the physical space.

    Design Principles

  • Build customer confidence both in-store and at home
  • Be relevant to contexts
  • Consider store associate-customer relationships
  • Provide a dip-in vs. zoned out interaction model, to prevent impeding tactile user experience

    • We started to create storyboard, and then sketched out detailed concepts. Some of my storyboards and sketches are in the following. I wireframed quickly and very low fidelity to explore different functionalities and interactions based on our design principles.

    Information Architecture

    We created a high level information architecture to guide our wireframing and paper prototyping.

    Mid-Fidelity Mockups

    After defining the information and functionalities, each of us explored different interactions and user interface first, and then evaluated them as a group. We also tried to add visual design to see if the content works. My selected mockups are as follows.

    Evaluation and Refinement

    After multiple iterations of low-fidelity paper prototypes, we moved directly to building a high fidelity interactive mobile prototype using Proto.io. Then, We took an iterative approach to our prototype evaluation and refinement. Firstly, we conducted an expert review with Alexander, Sr. UX Researcher from Microsoft. We then ealuated two users per round and iterating our designs between each round of evaluations. We triangulated our evaluation methods in order to capture both what our users were saying and doing, as well as both quantitative and quantitative measures.

  • In-store behavioral prototype (2): Understand what types of content and content qualities users found most helpful and inspiring.
  • Pre-interview using guided storytelling (4): Understand customer likelihood to engage in the experience and how well our concept resonates with users.
  • Scenario based, in-context usability testing with mobile prototype (4): Understand if users are able to complete core tasks on the application, and whether or not interactions in the store are hitting on predefined experience goals
  • Post-evaluation interview (4): Understand overall impression and experiential qualities of using the application in-store.

    Iteration 2 -- In-Store Evaluation

    Final Design

    Lumèire, a Sur La Table product companion app that inspires shoppers to up their cooking game by discovering tools that help them embark on new culinary endeavors.

    Lumèire leverages the unique in-store bazaar-like experience to promote discovery and helps Sur La Table turn foodies' obsession with tools into moments of inspiration and opportunity for education on tools and techniques. Inspiration and execution are brought together through curated photos and recipes in a single mobile application which is equally useful in store and at home.

    Lumiere Service Blueprint

    The service blueprint provides a snapshot of the crucial touch points Lumière has with customers and Sur La Table’s existing service system. Our biggest takeaway is that we must account for the supporting infrastructure of our system, and think through the necessary resource tradeoffs and opportunities to create value in the larger system.

    Competitive Opportunity

    Many of Sur La Table’s current touch points in the customer journey have addressed awareness, consideration, and perception out of the store. However, we feel there is a particular opportunity to fill the gap in the areas of in-store perception and the use phase, which follows purchase.

    Business Benefits

  • Success and confidence in the kitchen encourages customers to return to the store
  • Customers become brand ambassadors and content creators
  • More informed in-store customer engagement
  • Closes the gap between store and home
  • Insights into actual home use

  • Deliver content through a mobile app so content is accessible anywhere
  • Provide curated user-generated and professional photos, recipes, how-to videos
  • Facilitate meaningful sales associate interaction
  • Keep users immersed in store experience with simple, streamlined functionality

    Your dog is unhappy T^T

    It is really a problem for some of my teammates who have dogs. They are always very busy with their work and sometimes forget to walk their dogs. Though, I was unfamiliar with the dog walking service here, I was really curious, and wanted to make dogs happy:)

    We came up with our research questions, and then dived into research to find out the answers.

    1. Pet owners have difficulty finding service providers, particularly ones they feel they can trust, to provide services for their pets.
    2. Dog walkers have difficulty expanding their business because they are reliant on word of mouth.
    3. Trust in pet care providers is based on personal experience and the experience of a person’s social circle.
    4. Pet owners value social relationships with other pet owners.

    Why dog owners and dog walkers are also unhappy?

    Bridge dog owners and dog walkers.

    We found that there is a large gap in this market: No application exists to help dog owners find, hire, and book a dog walker.

    We created affinity diagrams to analyze our research, and then created personas in order to better empathize the target users.

    Design Process

    We analyzed our stakeholders and the service structure, after several ideations, heated-discussions, and sketched our ideas, we came up with our low-fidelity prototype.

    Iterate, iterate, iterate...

    We tested with three users for our paper prototype, and refined some design features.

    User testing tasks

    1. Logging in
    2. Choosing a dog to be walked
    3. Searching for and selecting a dog walker
    4. Scheduling a walk
    5. Canceling a scheduled walk

    Changes from paper prototype

    1. Added header with profile, schedule, and search links
    2. Added detailed walker info (price, distance, rating) on walker search page
    3. Added confirmation popup for walk deletion
    4. Added confirmation popup for walk scheduling
    5. Made method of deleting scheduled walks more obvious (added (-) button)

    Final Project

    present statistic data chart Sequentially VS Un-sequentially

    Collaborator: Christine Liu, Fork us on Github!

    Abstract

    The research focuses on the effectiveness of communicating a simple chart to audiences. The original hypothesis is that presenting a single statistic data chart sequentially works better than presenting it un-sequentially, in terms of the degree of understanding for audience. However, statistically speaking, there is no significant difference between presenting the chart sequentially and un-sequentially. We analyzed and discussed the reasons behind, and addressed our limitations and future work by the end of the paper.

    Visualization Design

    Antibiotics Visualization

    Design Rationale

    1.“lg(1/MIC)+3”represents the effectiveness of antibiotic

    MIC, minimum inhibitory concentration represents the concentration of antibiotic required to prevent growth in vitro, so that the more MIC, the less effective. The inverse ratio is a bit hard to be read from visual or non visual context. Humans tend to consider the bigger, the better. Thus, I used “1/MIC” instead of MIC, to represent the effectiveness of the antibiotic. The difference between some data (i.e. 870 almost 10000 times than 0.001) is so huge that cannot be shown very well. Thus, I used “lg(1/MIC)” to shorten the huge difference. In order to make the negative number into positive one and avoid any ambiguity, I added a “3” to “lg(1/MIC)”. Thus, “lg(1/MIC)+3” - the Y-axis represents the effectiveness of antibiotic. The higher or larger of lg(1/MIC)+3”, the more effective.

    2. I used three colors to differentiate three antibiotics, and used two transparent background colors to highlight different kinds of bacteria - positive and negative, thanks to which it is easier to tell whether a certain antibiotic is more effective for positive or negative bacteria.
